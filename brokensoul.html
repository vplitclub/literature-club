Broken Soul by Thomas Li

No matter how many times uncle Felix died, he just never seemed to stay buried. A lightning bolt fell from the sky and struck and recharged his dead body. As his bones reknitted, joints reconnected, he pondered. Why was the goddess of storms resurrecting him everytime he died? "Is this some eternal punishment for some crime I committed?" he hissed toward the sky. He could no longer feel the pain and discomfort of the revival process—he already felt it hundreds, if not thousands of times.

Felix sat up as he observed his surroundings. The land was lifeless, not a spark of green life. Instead of grass, the ground was littered with bodies. The hills were uneven, difficult to traverse. Yet, he walked through the carnage as if striding in a park. "Right... I last died during a battle." He bent down to the corpse of a knight cladded in plate armour. He pulled off the man's sword belt and strapped it to his own waist. Felix peeled the man's longsword from his fingers and sheathed it. He walked on, for he couldn't feel warmth nor cold, like a walking dead.

Before he saw them, he heard them. Plates of metal clanking as two legions of soldiers charged while screaming their battlecry, and Felix stood in the centre of the action. Holding his sword by the hilt, still sheathed, Felix ducked under the soldier's blade. Before the man could regain his guard, Felix pulled his blade from his sheath in a powerful bursting motion which pummelled the soldier square in the face. He flew back from the powerful blow, and before he could recover, Felix buried his blade in his neck.

***

The fighting did not stop for hours, not until those who lived had scattered or laid lifeless on the battlefield. Felix alone stood surrounded by a mountain of corpses. He looked within himself, for any emotion of disgust, bloodlust, sorrow, hate, or even pleasure, yet he only felt a wariness. A wariness that yearned for rest, an eternal sleep.

***

The sun was already setting when Felix walked through the tense, but lively streets of a small reinforcement town behind the front lines. The red clouds of dusk flooded the sky like blood spewed from a fatal wound. Candle lights appeared gradually, mirroring the flicker of stars in the heavens. The atmosphere bore a sense of dread and despair. “The enemy are pushing closer to this settlement day by day,” noted Felix, “it will be conquered in a matter of days.” Many shops were selling their last items before they evacuated. The citizens were on high alert, carrying as many necessities as possible and heading away from the front lines.

As Felix stumbled near the outskirts of the town he noticed a line of soldiers forming a blockade at the town’s entrance. ‘They must be desperate to draft civilians... and children.’ A small flicker of horror and rage awoke within his numb soul. ‘That was the age of my nephew when I saw him last—before he was drafted.’ As if the gods heard his thought, a young man in gleaming armour approached Felix. “Un-uncle Felix, is that you?” cried the young man. Uncle Felix felt an eternity had passed since he saw his nephew. Like waking from a nightmare, all emotions he could not feel before crashed against his soul like a great wave. His demeanour faltered and his mental walls cracked. His face, though motionless, bled streams of tears, cleansing his bloody and dirty face. His nephew approached him slowly and embraced him despite Felix’s ragged clothes.

“I—we thought you were dead! You went missing in action years ago!” his nephew sobbed.

“I’m sorry... I lost hope... I’m sorry...” Felix mumbled with tears, “I killed so many... innocent soldiers”.

“I am here for you uncle.”

“Thank you for allowing me to feel again.”

Felix held the embrace, shocked by the warmth of his body, the coolness of the air. He felt the blood that stained his hands, the guilt of every life he took. He felt it all. And the fatigue of life and yearning for death, he wished to never feel again.
